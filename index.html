Concept:

The following image is a sketch that represents the planned performance.
It would mean getting down on my knees in front of a central prayer object (intended as an altar, but in case that is impossible, something else can be built) and performing music written for the performance. I would have to move side to side slightly to control the sound devices, they would also not be too close together, the vision is to keep them slightly scatterd. During the entire performance I would be facing the "altar" object.


This is a more top down view.
The scale is slightly off, but represented better here than in the 1st image. There would be multiple instruments, connected by cables, and of course power cables. This is a part of the visual idea for it, the cables shouldnt be hidden.

Music by me:

This track was written as a commission for Samuels Ozoliņš' installation "When your body suddenly glitches right before falling asleep, isn't that the snake inside of your belly?" The installation (and thusly, the soundtrack) was inspired both by the snake theme in many mythologies as a wise yet evil animal and reflected on guilt induced by being a queer person coming from christian upbringing. This is of course a gross oversimplification of the art piece. Church theme is persistent in the track with the warped sound of church bells coming in and out at various points. It also loops perfectly, keeping the atmosphere uninterrupted while pushing and pulling on the viewer with raising and falling intensity and a touch of dissonance. The track combines many layers of both the bells (recorded and warped digitally) and many layers of analog synthesis to create a heavy dosage of volume - dissonant, all encompassing, powerful, like the feelings of guilt central to this work. I named the track "Odze", obviously after the snake.

This piece was written for an open compilation to raise money Southern California wildfire victims in 2025. It is a single track recorded in one go (albeit after a couple attempts to get it right) with minimal production. A very faint sound was layered in from a different take, I liked it and didn't want to leave it out. Multiple different sources feed through eachother (with some no-input mixing techniques and sequencing to create controlled feedback) and a delicate balance is achieved to create the tone. To me, it is heavy and viscous much like tar. The sound design draws from the idea of heavy, destructive fire and smoke. A strong inspiration was the heavy over-industrialization of our planet, wrecking it's ecosystems and balance of weather, that has caused excessive droughts causing wildfires not only in Cali.

I have worked on other commissions as well and performed a live soundtrack to accompany a performance, however those aren't recorded.

What I do:

I have been making music in various forms since late 2013. I now work in mostly what I would consider to be experimental music. My 2 main criteria for myself are probably a. make meaningful, thought through art and b. try to be as original as possible (of course we dont exist in vaccuum and so on, but I try). My current output utilizes sounds mostly generated by hardware - both digital and analog synthesizers and effects. I utilize unorthodox techniques, such as sequencing feedback. I am trying to find my ground in the more contemporary art related segments of sound, as I feel the most comfortable with those notions of what art generally is and I love to challenge the conventions of music. I come from a guitar background, so being able to replicate my music live without pre-recorded sounds is important to me when possible. My output is inspired by highly technical works like 90s idm and rhythmic and drone-y works like, well, drone, but also lowercase ambient and dub techno.

This is a copy of text I already submitted but just for reference I include it again:

The goal of this performance is to reflect on the complex relationship between, from one side, us as individuals with goals, values and world views born from our current understanding of the world and our way of life, and, from the other side, the heritage we receive and live through, and later pass down to future generations. In this case heritage is the very lived experience in actions and beliefs that becomes a part of us. Such heritage can live on through us directly, or show itself differently in execution but with the same meanings, or conversely - different meanings but similar actions.
This relationship exists in many forms, from newer generations finding themselves less religious or superstitious than previous ones, therefore having this interaction with religious rituals and values, to abuse victims harboring parts passed down to them from their abusers, or even our own past actions changing something within our behavior and continuing to live within us. The way that such heritage can live on even after purposefully separating yourself from its source is especially of interest to me in the creation of this piece. For example, such a situation is rather common in the queer community with some families being unable to accept their queer children, and the complex heritage a person would still carry from such a past experience and family, but this isn’t exclusive to queer people - complex relationships and heritage often form in many toxic families. This can be something light-hearted, like following traditions or rituals out of habit or comfort, or complex, like aspects of our personality we cherish or, conversely, deeply despise. The way this would be performed (on my knees, in work clothes) also symbolizes the way hard physical labor of the previous generations (and the experiences associated with such work) can live on through us. My father has worked in construction for a large part of his life so me wearing work pants and performing this is, in a way, a tribute to him and his heritage (dreams, trauma) living through me. These lived experiences of inheritance is what I’d attempt to explore in this performance.
These things are rarely gained by decision. Even though this heritage as such is essentially something inanimate, we don’t control it. We receive this heritage from others without their intention, and just as inadvertently we interact with it and pass it down. Heritage therefore exists similarly to an ever-present quantum field. It is seemingly set in stone the way it persists in itself, yet always moving and interacting. This is what the sonic aspects of the performance would be based on. In a purely practical sense this would be a form of a prayer consisting of experimental electronic music by using live musical instruments (synthesizers, drum machines) laid out in front of the altar, performed by me on my knees to imitate a form of prayer.
The performance closely fits the idea of heritage not being a static monument. The very ideas the performance touches are hard to define and pick apart, it is, to quote the description of the festival’s theme, “a continual process of remembering, rejecting, and re-imagining”. The performance reflects on the aspect of relating to trauma of previous generations living through us as something we never experienced directly, yet some parts of it still linger in not only our cognition, but in our very spirits. The performance lives through those aspects and, in a way, as pressures applied unintentionally by the past. This overall understanding of heritage reminds me much of the Higgs mechanism in quantum physics, the ever-present process giving everything mass. The Higgs field, mathematically, exists everywhere and we merely interact with it (this is of course a gross oversimplification, I am not a physician.) Heritage is much like that. We push and pull on it, and it pushes and pulls back on us. This very understanding of heritage (and expressions of it) mirror those expressed in the description of the festival: relating to “trauma and silences of those who came before”, the conflict between our beliefs, behavior and heritage, and the complex question of redefining it. This would also interact well with the central venue of the festival being a church and how religion can influence and become part of many aspects of this heritage.
At the start of the performance, the gear is already set up in front of the altar. I enter dressed in construction worker clothes (or similar work clothes), get on my knees facing the altar, do the sign of the cross with my hands, I then begin the performance controlling the synthesizers (and effects) by hand and play (and synthesize) sounds live. I remain on my knees for the entire performance. When I finish the performance, I sign the cross again.
The performance mirrors both the christian prayer and hard physical labor laying floors.
